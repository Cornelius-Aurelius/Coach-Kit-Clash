Coach Learning

Use this rule: the safest colour-blind kit decision is usually one team light and one team dark. Grey scale help removes colour names and shows light-vs-dark contrast. If two kits look similar in grey scale, they are likely to be confusing in real football conditions. The shirt is the main cue because players often only see a back view or side view at a glance.

Best colour choices

White vs black, blue vs yellow, red vs yellow, blue vs white and black vs yellow are strong beginner pairings because they create fast light-vs-dark separation.

Risky colour choices

Red/green/orange/brown, blue/purple/navy/black, yellow/orange, white/pastels and dark-vs-dark kits can all become confusing at speed or distance.

Why grey scale helps

Grey scale shows contrast without colour names. If two kits become similar in grey scale, they may be hard to separate during fast play, shade, floodlights, rain or bright sun.

Why it affects play

Colour-blind players may need extra time to identify teammates. At speed, they often see only the back or side of a shirt, moving in and out of shade, so weak contrast can cause hesitation, wrong passes and lost confidence.
